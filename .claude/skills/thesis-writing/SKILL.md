# Skill: thesis-writing

## Thesis Writing Goals

The primary goal is to write the diploma thesis as **substantive and voluminous as possible**, while maintaining strict academic integrity:

- **Maximize content**: prefer longer, more detailed explanations, richer literature reviews, fuller methodology descriptions, and thorough discussion of results. More is better, as long as it is accurate.
- **No fabrication**: never invent facts, results, quotes, or claims that are not supported by cited sources. Every empirical claim must have a real citation.
- **No stretching**: do not attribute claims to a paper unless that paper actually makes those claims. Do not use a citation just because the topic is loosely related.
- **Correct citations are non-negotiable**: if a citation is wrong, either find the correct source or rewrite the text without the unsupported claim. Removing a sentence is better than keeping a false citation.
- **Expand, don't cut**: when fixing a wrong citation, try to find a correct replacement source and expand the discussion rather than deleting the paragraph.

---

## Hard Rules

When editing or writing thesis content, follow these rules strictly:

1. Do not read the entire thesis at once.
2. Do not recursively inspect `Diploma_latex/` folder as a whole.
3. Only read small, targeted snippets from LaTeX files when explicitly needed for a focused task.
4. Treat `Diploma_latex/` as the source of truth for the diploma text, but not as a folder for broad exploration.
5. Keep edits minimal and scoped.
6. Write all content in English (for thesis), Russian (for documentation and notes).

---

## Why These Rules Exist

- **Scope limitation**: reading the entire thesis at once wastes context and causes unfocused decisions.
- **Integrity over expansion**: a fabricated page is worse than no page. Academic honesty is the foundation.
- **Citation as constraint**: citations are not decoration — they are the paper trail that makes claims credible. A claim without support is just an opinion.
- **Expand with sources**: if something seems important but isn't cited, the answer is "find the source," not "stretch an existing citation."

---

## Working Rhythm

1. **Start focused**: know exactly what section or claim you're checking/writing before opening a file.
2. **Target narrowly**: read only the relevant excerpt, not the whole chapter.
3. **Cite as you write**: do not write a paragraph and hunt for citations later — the citation comes first.
4. **Verify**: use skills like `check-citations` and `check-context` to validate bibliography entries.
5. **Expand, don't shrink**: when you hit a citation problem, try to find a better source and rewrite richer, not delete.
