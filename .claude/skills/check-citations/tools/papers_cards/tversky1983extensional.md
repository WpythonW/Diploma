## Goal
The paper aims to investigate how people make probability judgments and to demonstrate that human reasoning often deviates from the norms of probability theory, particularly through the conjunction fallacy. It seeks to contrast extensional reasoning, which adheres to formal rules of logic and probability, with intuitive reasoning, which relies on heuristics and is prone to systematic biases. The authors explore the implications of these biases for understanding cognitive processes in judgment under uncertainty.

## Gap Addressed
Prior research had identified discrepancies between human judgment and probability theory, but there was limited systematic analysis of why people consistently violate basic probability rules like the conjunction principle. The conjunction fallacy—where individuals judge a conjunction (e.g., "Linda is a bank teller and a feminist") as more probable than one of its constituents—posed a challenge to rational models of reasoning. This paper fills the gap by providing empirical evidence and a theoretical framework explaining this fallacy through the use of representativeness and other judgment heuristics.

## Method
The authors conducted a series of experiments in which participants were asked to assess the likelihood of various events, including single propositions and their conjunctions, often using personality sketches (e.g., the famous "Linda problem"). They manipulated the representativeness of outcomes and measured whether participants' probability judgments violated the conjunction rule. The studies compared intuitive judgments with normative standards from probability theory, analyzing patterns across different contexts and populations to isolate the role of heuristics in reasoning errors.

## Datasets and Metrics
**Datasets:** The studies used original experimental data collected from human participants, including university students and other adult samples, though specific demographic details are not emphasized. Stimuli included hypothetical scenarios (e.g., personality descriptions) and probabilistic judgment tasks designed to elicit the conjunction fallacy.

**Metrics:** Probability ratings or rankings of event likelihoods; frequency of conjunction fallacy responses; consistency with extensional (logical) probability principles; degree of reliance on representativeness heuristic.

## Results
Participants consistently judged conjunctions as more probable than their constituent single events, violating the basic laws of probability. For example, in the Linda problem, most subjects judged "Linda is a bank teller and a feminist" as more likely than "Linda is a bank teller," despite the logical impossibility. These errors were robust across variations in wording, context, and population. The findings indicate that people rely on intuitive heuristics—especially representativeness—rather than extensional reasoning when making probability judgments. The conjunction fallacy was more prevalent when the added detail made the scenario seem more plausible or representative, even though it reduced its actual probability. This pattern supports the hypothesis that intuitive judgments are guided by similarity and narrative coherence rather than probabilistic logic.

## Limitations
The study relies heavily on hypothetical scenarios, which may not reflect real-world decision-making. The generalizability of findings is limited by the use of convenience samples, primarily consisting of students. Additionally, the paper does not fully address how context, feedback, or training might mitigate the observed biases.
