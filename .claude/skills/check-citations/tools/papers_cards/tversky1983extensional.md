## Goal
The authors aimed to investigate how people make probability judgments and to demonstrate systematic deviations from the laws of probability, particularly focusing on the conjunction fallacy. They sought to contrast extensional reasoning—governed by formal rules of probability—with intuitive reasoning, which relies on heuristics and mental shortcuts. The paper explores how human judgment often violates the basic principle that a conjunction of events cannot be more probable than either of its components. Through a series of experiments, the authors examined the conditions under which people incorrectly judge the likelihood of conjunctive events, especially when narrative coherence or representativeness influences perception.
