## Goal
The book aims to explore how human reasoning is influenced by the structure and content of problems, emphasizing the cognitive mechanisms underlying logical inference and the systematic biases that affect it. It investigates the discrepancy between normative models of reasoning and actual human performance, particularly in deductive tasks. The authors seek to understand how people interpret logical statements and why they often deviate from formal logic in predictable ways.

## Gap Addressed
Prior research in logic and philosophy assumed reasoning followed formal rules, but little was known about how people actually reason in practice. There was a lack of empirical investigation into the psychological processes governing reasoning, especially regarding the roles of linguistic structure and contextual content. This book addresses that gap by presenting experimental evidence on reasoning behavior and identifying systematic errors, such as confirmation bias.

## Method
The authors employ experimental paradigms, most notably the Wason selection task, to study human reasoning. They present participants with logical problems involving conditional statements and examine how variations in content and context influence performance. The method combines theoretical analysis with empirical testing to uncover cognitive principles underlying reasoning errors and strategies.

## Results
The research reveals that people consistently fail to adhere to formal logic in reasoning tasks, particularly when abstract content is involved. Performance improves significantly when reasoning problems are framed in familiar or meaningful contexts, suggesting content heavily influences logical judgment. The selection task demonstrates a robust tendency toward confirmation bias—participants tend to seek confirming evidence rather than disconfirming evidence. These findings laid the foundation for dual-process theories of reasoning and influenced cognitive psychology and behavioral sciences.

## Limitations
The experiments often use small, non-diverse samples, limiting generalizability. The focus on specific tasks like the selection task may not fully represent the breadth of human reasoning in real-world contexts.
