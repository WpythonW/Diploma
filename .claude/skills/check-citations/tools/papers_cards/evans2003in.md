## Goal
The paper aims to present and evaluate dual-process theories of human reasoning, which propose that there are two distinct cognitive systems underlying thinking: one fast, automatic, and intuitive (System 1), and another slower, controlled, and reflective (System 2). It seeks to integrate empirical findings from reasoning research into a coherent theoretical framework. The goal is to clarify the nature, origins, and implications of these two systems for understanding rationality and cognitive functioning.

## Gap Addressed
Prior to this review, there was a lack of comprehensive synthesis of the growing body of evidence supporting dual-process accounts across different domains of reasoning and judgment. Many studies had identified anomalies in logical reasoning that traditional models could not explain, suggesting the need for a more nuanced account. The paper addresses inconsistencies in terminology and theoretical framing, aiming to unify disparate findings under a single dual-process framework.

## Method
The author conducts a conceptual review and theoretical analysis of existing literature on reasoning, judgment, and decision-making. Evidence from experimental psychology, including studies on syllogistic reasoning, belief bias, and heuristic thinking, is interpreted through the lens of dual-process theory. The paper distinguishes between two cognitive systems based on their processing characteristics, such as speed, capacity, and susceptibility to bias.

## Results
The paper successfully outlines a robust dual-process framework that explains a wide range of reasoning phenomena, such as belief bias and the dissociation between logical and intuitive responses. It demonstrates how System 1 generates rapid, heuristic-based judgments, while System 2 supports effortful, rule-based reasoning. The model accounts for individual differences in cognitive ability and thinking dispositions. It has become foundational in cognitive science, influencing research in psychology, behavioral economics, and artificial intelligence. The article is highly cited, indicating its significant impact on the field.

## Limitations
The theory is largely descriptive and lacks detailed mechanistic explanations of how the two systems interact. It does not fully specify the conditions under which System 2 overrides System 1, nor does it provide a precise neurocognitive architecture for the proposed systems.
