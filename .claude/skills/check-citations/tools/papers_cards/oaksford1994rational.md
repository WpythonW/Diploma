## Goal
The paper aims to provide a rational explanation for human performance on the Wason selection task, a classic problem in cognitive psychology that reveals apparent reasoning errors. The authors argue that people's seemingly irrational choices can be understood as optimal data selection under uncertainty. By applying principles of rational analysis, they propose that human reasoning is adapted to the structure of the environment and the probabilistic nature of real-world information. The goal is to reframe the selection task not as a test of logical competence, but as a problem of optimal information gathering.

## Gap Addressed
Traditional interpretations of the Wason selection task assume that human reasoning is systematically flawed because participants rarely select the logically correct cards. However, these accounts fail to explain why people consistently select the cards they do across variations of the task. There is a lack of a principled, predictive framework that explains when and why people’s selections deviate from classical logic. The authors identify a need for a theory that treats reasoning as an adaptive process optimized for real-world conditions rather than abstract logical norms.

## Method
The authors apply rational analysis—a method that models cognitive processes as optimal solutions to environmental problems—to the selection task. They model the task as a problem of optimal data selection, where participants aim to maximize expected information gain under probabilistic assumptions about the rule and the world. Using Bayesian principles, they derive predictions about card selection frequencies based on the informativeness of evidence. The model incorporates factors such as prior beliefs, the rarity of properties (the "rarity assumption"), and the utility of different types of evidence.

## Results
The rational analysis model successfully predicts the most commonly selected cards (e.g., P and Q) as those that are most informative under realistic assumptions about the world. It explains why people rarely select the logically correct but often uninformative cards (e.g., not-Q). The model accounts for shifts in selection patterns when task conditions change, such as when rule probabilities or card frequencies are manipulated. It also explains why thematic content improves performance—because it makes the relevant probabilities more transparent. The account outperforms existing theories by providing a unified, quantitative explanation of a wide range of findings.

## Limitations
The model relies on assumptions about prior probabilities and utility that are difficult to measure independently. It does not fully address individual differences in reasoning or the role of cognitive architecture constraints. Additionally, while it explains patterns of choices, it does not directly model the cognitive mechanisms that implement the optimal strategy.
