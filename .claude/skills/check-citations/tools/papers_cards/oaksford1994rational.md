## Goal
The authors aimed to explain human performance on the Wason selection task—a classic problem in cognitive psychology—by proposing that people's seemingly irrational choices are actually rational when viewed through the lens of optimal data selection. They argue that human reasoning is adapted to the structure of the environment, particularly the assumption that rare events are more informative when testing hypotheses. By applying a Bayesian framework, the paper seeks to demonstrate that participants' selections align with a rational strategy for gathering diagnostic evidence under conditions of uncertainty and limited information. The goal is to reframe apparent reasoning errors as optimal behavior given probabilistic assumptions about real-world data.

## Gap Addressed
Prior accounts of the Wason selection task, such as the original logical interpretation by Peter Wason, treated participants' frequent failure to select logically correct cards as evidence of flawed reasoning. Subsequent theories proposed various heuristic or content-based explanations (e.g., permission schemas or social contracts), but lacked a general, principled account of why certain cards are selected across different contexts. The gap addressed is the absence of a normative, domain-general model that explains both typical patterns of responding and how they might reflect rational inference processes. Oaksford and Chater identify this theoretical shortfall and propose a rational analysis framework that fills it by modeling data selection as an optimization problem under uncertainty.

## Method
The authors apply Anderson’s framework of rational analysis to model human hypothesis testing in the selection task. They develop a Bayesian model that calculates the expected utility of selecting each card based on information gain, using the assumption that evidence is more valuable when it pertains to rare events (the "rarity assumption"). The model assigns higher expected informativeness to cards corresponding to less frequent outcomes, thereby predicting which cards people should select if they are maximizing expected information gain. This approach contrasts with classical logic-based predictions and instead offers a probabilistic account of optimal data selection, grounded in statistical inference principles.

## Results
The Bayesian model successfully predicts that participants will preferentially select the P and not-Q cards in the standard selection task, which matches typical human response patterns. It explains the modal selection of the P card as rational because it corresponds to a rare outcome and thus carries more potential information. The model accounts for shifts in card selection when problem context or probability assumptions change, outperforming purely logical or syntactic accounts. Empirical results from previous studies are reinterpreted as consistent with optimal data selection under rarity assumptions, suggesting that human reasoning is not flawed but adapted to real-world environments where rare events are more diagnostic. The model also generates novel predictions for performance under varying base rates, later confirmed in follow-up experiments.

## Limitations
The model depends on assumptions about prior probabilities and environmental structure, which may not always be known or stable across contexts. It does not fully account for all context effects observed in later studies, particularly those involving deontic reasoning or social exchange, which some researchers argue require additional mechanisms beyond probabilistic optimization.
