## Goal
The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) produces the Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) program to provide detailed, reliable estimates of employment and wages for approximately 830 occupations across the United States. The goal is to support workforce planning, policy development, education and training programs, and economic research by delivering granular labor market data at national, state, metropolitan, and industry levels. The data aims to reflect current labor market conditions using standardized occupational and industry classifications. The May 2024 estimates serve as the most recent snapshot of occupational employment and wage trends.

## Gap Addressed
Prior to the OEWS program, occupational employment data was less detailed, less frequent, or limited in geographic or occupational coverage. Existing labor market surveys often lacked the granularity or consistency needed for state and local workforce decision-making. The OEWS fills this gap by offering annually updated, model-based estimates derived from a large sample of employer surveys, standardized under the 2018 Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) and 2022 North American Industry Classification System (NAICS). This enables consistent cross-regional and cross-industry comparisons that many prior datasets could not support.

## Method
The OEWS program uses a semiannual survey of approximately 1.1 million business establishments across the U.S., collected through the BLS’s establishment survey program. Employment and wage data are collected for all workers in sampled establishments, classified by occupation and industry. Estimates are produced using a model-based methodology that combines three years of survey data (e.g., May 2022, 2023, and 2024 for the 2024 estimates) to improve reliability, especially for smaller geographic areas or rare occupations. The data are benchmarked to employment totals from the Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW) and adjusted for nonresponse and coverage.

## Datasets and Metrics
**Datasets:** The OEWS program uses raw survey responses from approximately 1.1 million employer establishments collected semiannually. The final published dataset includes employment and wage estimates for about 830 occupations across all 50 states, the District of Columbia, Puerto Rico, and numerous metropolitan and nonmetropolitan areas. The estimates are based on a pooled dataset combining survey responses from May 2022, May 2023, and May 2024. Supporting data include QCEW employment totals for benchmarking.

**Metrics:** The primary metrics include mean and median hourly and annual wages, employment levels per occupation, and employment per thousand jobs. Confidence intervals and relative standard errors are provided in technical documentation to indicate estimate reliability. For example, the national mean annual wage across all occupations was $61,690 in May 2024. Specific occupations report detailed percentiles (10th, 25th, 50th, 75th, 90th) for wage distributions.

## Results
The May 2024 OEWS data release includes comprehensive national, state, and metropolitan-level estimates for 830 occupations. For example, the national median annual wage was $49,760, while the mean was $61,690, highlighting wage distribution skew. High-wage occupations such as surgeons and anesthesiologists exceeded $280,000 in mean annual wages, while service occupations like fast food workers averaged around $28,000. State-level data revealed significant geographic variation—for instance, mean annual wages for software developers ranged from $95,000 in some states to over $150,000 in others. The data are widely used by policymakers, educators, and job seekers for labor market analysis.

## Limitations
The OEWS estimates are model-based and rely on three years of pooled data, which may delay the reflection of sudden labor market shifts. Self-employed workers are excluded from the estimates, limiting coverage in certain industries. Additionally, some small geographic areas or rare occupations have higher margins of error due to smaller sample sizes, and the data are not designed to capture short-term fluctuations.
