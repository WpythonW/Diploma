## Goal
The paper aims to provide a comprehensive review and theoretical framework for dual-process accounts of human reasoning. It seeks to clarify the distinction between two types of cognitive processes—fast, intuitive reasoning and slower, deliberative reasoning—and explain how these systems interact in shaping human thought and decision-making. The author explores the implications of this duality across various domains of reasoning, including logical, probabilistic, and moral judgments. The scope includes integrating psychological evidence into a coherent model that explains both rational and biased patterns of thinking.

## Gap Addressed
Prior to this work, there was a lack of unified theoretical structure to explain conflicting patterns in human reasoning—such as when people succeed in logical tasks versus when they exhibit persistent biases. Existing models often treated reasoning as a unitary process, failing to account for the coexistence of heuristic-based and rule-based inference. Evans addresses this by synthesizing earlier fragmented ideas (e.g., from cognitive psychology and social cognition) into a formal dual-process framework, building on but distinguishing from earlier proposals like those of Wason and Johnson-Laird.

## Method
The paper employs a theoretical and integrative approach, synthesizing empirical findings from decades of reasoning research into a dual-process model. It distinguishes System 1 (fast, automatic, implicit, evolutionarily ancient) from System 2 (slow, analytic, explicit, linked to working memory and general intelligence). The method involves analyzing patterns of reasoning performance across tasks (e.g., syllogistic reasoning, belief bias paradigms) to argue that both systems operate in parallel and often compete. The author uses evidence from experimental psychology to support the claim that reasoning is governed by two distinct cognitive systems.

## Results
The paper presents a robust theoretical case for dual-process theory, supported by extensive experimental evidence showing systematic conflicts between intuitive (System 1) and analytical (System 2) reasoning. It demonstrates that people often default to heuristic-based judgments (System 1), even when these conflict with logical norms, but can override them with effortful reasoning (System 2) under certain conditions. The model explains phenomena such as belief bias, where logically valid conclusions are rejected if they conflict with prior beliefs. This framework became foundational in cognitive science, influencing fields like behavioral economics, moral psychology, and AI. The paper has been cited over 2,000 times, indicating significant impact.

## Limitations
The paper acknowledges that the dual-process model is descriptive rather than fully computational, leaving open questions about how the two systems interact mechanistically. It does not specify precise boundary conditions for when System 1 versus System 2 dominates, nor does it fully address individual differences in reasoning dispositions.
